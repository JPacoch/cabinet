## Husserl

The beginnings of the twentieth century really entangled philosophers in a peculiar state of paralysis. Many discussions no matter how local, or global, practical or theoretical, risked being dissolved into a somewhat tragic, the same final matter of “Is the world even real? How could we know for certain? What is the relation between that real world and a human mind?”. All the legitimate, somehow inevitable questions,yet fatal to any pragmatic discussion. When every issue collapses into the foundational dilemma, none could be resolved really. 

Husserl’s phenomenology was a methodological breakthrough. *Epoche* - the suspension of any assumptions (including metaphysical ones) was one of the finest inventions. Yet, Husserl did not deny the world, nor declared metaphysics obsolete, nonfactual, a fairy. He proposed a tool, a way of setting aside any prejudices, biases, dilemmas and worldviews temporarily in order to examine what is given in the experience, as it appears, and analyse the phenomenon further in that manner. The universe itself is not rejected, rather placed between (or behind) the brackets so that human consciousness, experience, meaning and appearance could be studied without distractions. 

I believe Husserl’s intentions were of a pragmatic nature, very epistemology-centered. He sought clarity in that process of dissection and analyses, not an ultimate worldview or holistic picture of how things really are. One could think of it rather like metaphysics is being postponed, not abolished. Bracketing is thorough and strategic, but temporary and most importantly - limited. It is a suspension for the sake of focus, one could not conclude from this that nothing for certain lies beyond the bracket. 

Husserl’s tool allows us to examine the gallery paintings without really thinking about where the gallery building is located, what are its foundations made of, and which side of the world the main entrance is facing. Most importantly, by the use of phenomenology none of us is bothered by the question whether that art gallery exists at all, yet that phenomenology itself does not claim that those questions do not matter.

What may be bothering is that many thinkers after Husserl misused that tool to create from it an ontology of its own. 


## Sartre

Sartre inherits Husserl's method but fundamentally transforms the scope of it. What was meant to be just a method in frenchmen’s hands becomes a complex picture of reality. Sartre’s phenomenology, especially in *Being and Nothingness*, presents more as an extensive ontology. He describes consciousness, the *for-itself*, the *in-itself*, freedom, nothingness, and the structure of human existence like a cartographer mapping a continent that is fully accessible with use of pure experience. So he does not use phenomenology to analyze the reality, rather speaks of his observations as they were exhausting the whole picture of what there is. A tool becomes a worldview. As Sartre says in *Being and Nothingness*:

>"Ontology itself can be attempted only as a phenomenology."

His identification of phenomenological description as ontology is precisely where the problem arises. He never recovers from the *epoche* and stays in the bracket, moreover his whole universe is built within it. If we were to freeze that viewpoint I would argue that this suspension, that *epoch*e from metaphysical plain becomes a metaphysical claim itself - for Sartre what lies beyond human experience and appearance is philosophically irrelevant. In his world the universe in essence is just an insignificant background for human existence. Everything apart from his concept of *for-itself* (and the actions revolving around it) is really diminished to the role of *in-itself* - muted, grayed out, secondary, unimportant.

This results in a creation of a claustrophobic system, in which human perspective becomes the axis around which everything else - including ontology - revolves. The complexity of what lies beyond the experience is silently omitted. Not negated, not refuted, not examined - neglected and left out.

In that context, what was meant for us to help us see clearer (phenomenology) becomes a boundary of what was ever allowed to be seen.

## The mistake

Husserl’s bracketing was never meant to define the limits of reality, yet Sartre treats it as if it does. He speaks of phenomenological appearances as if they were ontological structures, I would call that a category error. The method of describing experiences does not cover the totality of what there ultimately is.

However, Sartre’s viewpoint does not eliminate metaphysics, rather produces an accidental one. To rely exclusively on what appears to us is itself a leap of faith, no more no less than the leap needed to reject or affirm religious belief. This assumes that appearance, pictured by human consciousness, is sufficient enough to create and ground ontologies, a whole system to be precise. It’s an unnoticed, neglected but very real metaphysical commitment. 

Here, I would argue, works of Kant and Husserl are often misinterpreted or not understood fully. Neither of them claimed to have resolved the structure and outlines of reality; Kant limited knowledge to phenomena, yet insisted on existence of the noumenal. Husserl limited scope of analysis to appearance, but for the sake of clarity, not as a way to claim that appearance is the totality. Sartre acts as if those limits were the edge of the knowledgeable world (or rather what edge of what there is meaningful in it). 

This of course leads to human-centered ontology, where human is not only the only essence of meaning but really the only lens through which meaning is really seen and existent. The rest - what lies beyond the field of view of that lens - is just a noise. So one cannot say Sartre disposes of that part of reality, there is something besides what there is to be viewed through the lens - he just leaves it unaddressed.

## The call

What is needed is not rejection of phenomenology, but a proper use of it. Husserl’s work remains invaluable. It allows focused inquiry and protects philosophy from endless doubts and debates that quake the ground of every new discussion. However, phenomenology must remain what it was intended to be - a tool, with all its limits, flaws and burdens. 

We need philosophies that can use that tool without being enclosed by it, philosophies that examine experience but still remain open for what there is beyond, philosophies that postpone metaphysics but do not settle on that.

Phenomenology should assist us in seeing further, not limit us on what we are allowed to see. It is a starting point of an analysis, not its final boundary. And to be faithful to its task, after it is being used, the *epoche* must be set aside.

Husserl gave us a discipline of thought, Sartre turned it into a universe. The task now is to restore the original proportion. 
And if phenomenology refuses to step outside of itself, then isn't it becoming the very thing it was meant to battle?
